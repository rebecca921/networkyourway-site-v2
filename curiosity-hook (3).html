The Curiosity
Hook.

Build a version of what you do that travels well in a room. This isn't a pitch. It's a conversation starter — three parts that make the right person lean in and ask the follow-up question.

What this is

The goal isn't to impress.
It's to make them curious.

Most people answer "what do you do?" with a job title. The right person hears it and moves on. The Curiosity Hook gives you a different answer — one that makes the right person think tell me more about that.

It works because it's specific, not broad. It speaks to a frustration, not a feature. And it leaves a door open instead of closing with a pitch.

"The more specific you are, the more the right person will lean in — and the wrong person will politely move on. Both outcomes are a win."

Three parts. Fill in each honestly. Then read it back out loud. If it sounds like you — it's ready.

The framework

Three parts.
One conversation starter.

1
Who You Help
Be specific, not broad

Resist the urge to say "anyone who..." — that tells nobody anything. The more specific you are, the more the right person will lean in. Think about the person you do your best work with. Name them.

Sector, role, situation — any of these work. The point is to create a moment of recognition: that's me or I know someone like that.

Example "I work with operations managers in professional services firms who are..."
2
The Problem You Solve
Say it the way they feel it

Not the solution — the frustration. Use the words they'd actually say at 11pm on a Sunday when it's all going round in their head. This is the part most people get wrong — they describe what they do, not what it feels like to need it.

If they heard you say this across a room, they should feel seen — not sold to.

Example "...brilliant at what they do but keep being overlooked for the next step — doing everything right and still being invisible..."
3
The Open Door
Make them want to ask more

One sentence. No close, no pitch. Just enough to make the right person think: actually, tell me more about that. This is where most people overdo it — they add the outcome, the method, the case study. Don't. Leave the door open.

The follow-up question is the goal. That's when the real conversation starts.

Example "Most people I work with say they'd been approaching it completely wrong — and once they saw why, everything shifted quite fast."
